# Ecommerce Business Review

> Revenue reached $8.6M for the year but was essentially flat YoY (-2.3%), masking a dramatic shift underneath: new customer acquisition collapsed 57.7% while the returning customer base expanded nearly 4x. Short-term momentum is strong — the last 90 days surged 87% on Q4 seasonal demand — but this growth is built entirely on an aging customer base and aging catalog (60% of SKUs in decline stage). The most urgent priority is relaunching acquisition channels to replenish the customer pipeline before retention gains plateau.

## 90d Momentum: Seasonal surge lifting all metrics, but AOV is compressing

```
Revenue $3.46M (vs prior 90d: +87.4%)
|-- New Customer Revenue $405K (11.7% of total)
|   |-- New Customers: 597 (+96.4%)
|   |-- New Customer AOV: $381
|-- Returning Customer Revenue $3.05M (88.3% of total)
    |-- Returning Customers: 2,292 (+44.0%)
    |-- Returning AOV: $548
```

**Growth Drivers:** The 90d surge was overwhelmingly volume-driven (+$1.26M from order count growth) with a meaningful AOV contribution (+$352K). Both new and returning customers expanded significantly, reflecting Q4 seasonal demand pulling buyers back into the funnel.

### AOV eroding under volume surge

**What is:** AOV dropped 18.8% MoM (from $539 in Oct to $437 in Nov), the steepest single-month decline in the dataset.

**Why it matters:** Despite order count surging 37.7% MoM, the AOV compression means November's revenue per order was $100 lower than October's. If order growth normalizes post-season while AOV stays depressed, the $266K annualized AOV gap will translate directly into revenue loss.

**What to do:** Analyze the category mix shift between Oct and Nov to determine whether lower-AOV products drove the volume increase, and set a free-shipping threshold at $400 to anchor basket sizes.

### Seasonal concentration building

**What is:** Sep-Nov generated $3.15M — 37% of annual revenue in just three months, with monthly averages of $1.05M vs $583K for Jan-Aug.

**Why it matters:** The Q4 ramp is 80% above the Jan-Aug baseline, but the business has no comparable demand lever in the first half of the year. Any Q4 shortfall — supply disruption, competitive pressure, or macro headwind — would leave a gap that H1 cannot absorb.

**What to do:** Build a mid-year demand initiative (summer campaign, loyalty event, or product launch cadence) to reduce Q4 dependency below 30% of annual revenue.

## 365d Structure: Flat revenue masks a fundamental acquisition-to-retention imbalance

```
Revenue $8.6M (YoY: -2.3%)
|-- New Customer Revenue $1.75M (20.3% of total)
|   |-- New Customers: 1,566 (-57.7%)
|   |-- New Customer AOV: $432
|-- Returning Customer Revenue $6.85M (79.7% of total)
    |-- Returning Customers: 2,698 (+391.4%)
    |-- Returning AOV: $500
    |-- Repeat Purchase Rate: 72.4%
```

**Growth Drivers:** Revenue was pulled in opposite directions — volume contracted (-$321K from fewer orders) while AOV provided a small offset (+$119K). The net result is near-flat revenue that obscures a complete structural inversion: the business shifted from acquisition-led to retention-dependent in a single year.

### New customer acquisition collapsed

**What is:** New customer count fell 57.7% YoY (from ~3,700 to 1,566), and new customer revenue share dropped to 20.3%.

**Why it matters:** The 72.4% repeat purchase rate and 5-day median time-to-second-purchase are exceptional — well above D2C benchmarks. However, even the strongest retention engine needs fresh customers entering the top of the funnel. At the current rate of decline, the returning customer base will begin shrinking within 2-3 quarters as natural churn outpaces new-to-repeat conversion.

**What to do:** Reactivate acquisition channels with dedicated budget, separate from retention spend, targeting a return to 200+ new customers per month.

### Catalog aging — 60% of products in decline stage

**What is:** 60.2% of active SKUs are classified as decline-stage based on lifecycle analysis, far above the 30% threshold.

**Why it matters:** Despite this, the catalog still functions effectively as a bundle engine — multi-item order rate is 91.7% with 238 strong cross-sell pairs. But this basket strength depends on breadth. As decline-stage products lose velocity, the cross-sell network thins and basket sizes will follow, compounding the AOV compression already visible in the 90d data.

**What to do:** Prioritize new product development for the top 20 decline-stage SKUs by revenue contribution, using existing cross-sell data to inform which product adjacencies to fill first.

### Revenue concentrated in top customer segment

**What is:** Top 10% of customers generate 63.9% of total revenue (above the 60% watch threshold).

**Why it matters:** Combined with the acquisition collapse, the high-value customer segment is not being replenished at the rate it naturally churns. The business effectively depends on ~430 customers for nearly two-thirds of revenue — a concentration level that creates material risk from any change in a small number of accounts.

**What to do:** Develop a mid-tier customer growth program that moves customers from occasional to regular buyers, reducing dependency on the top segment while maintaining champion engagement.
